Imagery Brief — Philosophy, Per-Screen Slots, and File Naming for Claude Code
Core principle: Images must show the gap, not the scenery. Every cruise line already publishes beautiful landscape photography. The Host Atlas images show a human in the frame, facing away or looking out, and a landscape beyond them that has no label, no famous landmark, no caption. The image performs the silent stretch — it doesn't illustrate it. Deck-level, human-scale, unnamed world beyond. No drones. No postcard angles. No famous geography.
River cruise Sundeck, afternoon. One figure at the railing from behind. Vine-covered hills or unnamed valley beyond. Slightly underexposed — overcast or soft afternoon light preferred. No castle. No famous gorge. The ordinary stretch between the headline moments.
Coastal cruise Balcony or deck rail, early morning or dusk. Cliffs or coastline sliding by. Coffee cup on the rail optional. Phone face-down on a table. The coast has no caption. No port. No landmark. Just rock and water and silence.
Expedition cruise Zodiac at mid-distance crossing still dark water toward an unnamed shore — OR — a figure at a ship bow, expedition jacket, approaching something the viewer cannot identify. The approach matters more than the destination.
Verbatim callout treatment: Verbatims appear as floating callout boxes over landscape images — not as text blocks between paragraphs. Each box: warm off-white background, 0.5px bronze border, Playfair italic inside. Below the box: a thin bronze vertical line descending to a small bronze dot anchored to a feature in the landscape (a hillside, a ruin, a vineyard slope). The verbatim appears to comment on the unnamed thing the guest is looking at. Think margin note in a beautifully printed book — not a speech bubble.

Problem statement — three paragraphs · build the commercial case
Travel operators cover the highlights well. Guides bring the expected moments to life for the whole group — the famous landmarks, the signature ports, the views people came to see. That part is handled with care and knowledge.
What no one has designed for is everything between. The long transit between cities. The coast sliding by at dusk. The river valley that holds five centuries of history with not a word of explanation. 60 to 80 percent of many journeys passes in scenic silence.
Guests are still curious during these stretches. They want to understand what they are seeing. Without context, scenery becomes wallpaper. Attention fades. The question forms — and finds no answer. When information does exist, it often arrives too late. By the time an announcement is made, the moment has passed — the castle has slipped behind the bend.

Individual curation — design and copy note: This is the most strategically important screen on the site. It closes the implicit objection every operator has: "can't my guide just do this?" The answer is not a criticism of guides — it is a structural fact. A guide speaks to the group. One voice, one story, one pace, for the whole deck, simultaneously, in one language. The Host Atlas speaks to each guest individually — in their language, at their pace, around their own curiosity. Two guests looking at the same valley may be following entirely different stories. That is not possible with a microphone and a group. It is the nature of a personal lens. The copy should make this feel generous toward guides — they own the highlights, and they are excellent at it. The Host Atlas works in the space the guide format cannot reach.

Body — Inter 18px · the individual argument · concrete before abstract
Two guests stand at the same railing, looking at the same stretch of valley. One notices the rows of vines on the slope — who planted them, what variety, whose label ends up on the bottle. The other notices the earthworks on the hillside above — the outline of something older, something that predates the vineyard by eight centuries.
A guide with a microphone can tell one story to the group. The Host Atlas can tell both stories simultaneously — to different guests, in different languages, at different depths.
